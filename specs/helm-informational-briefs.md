# HELM — How It Works: Seven Briefs
## For prospective users and curious friends
## Draft: June 2026

---

## BRIEF 1: What Is HELM?

HELM is a personal platform for building tools, running automations, and managing information — all through plain English conversations.

It's not a chatbot you ask questions. It's not a to-do list app. It's closer to having a tireless team working behind the scenes, 24/7:

- Building software tools customized exactly to how you think and work
- Monitoring what matters to you and surfacing what's changed
- Sending you a morning briefing before you've checked your phone
- Remembering everything you've shared — and connecting the dots over time
- Getting better at serving you the longer it runs

The core experience: you talk to HELM through Discord — the same app many people use for gaming or communities — and it handles the rest. Your Discord server becomes your personal command center.

---

## BRIEF 2: The Command Center (Discord)

HELM lives in a private Discord server that belongs only to you.

Each channel has a purpose:

- #general — where you make requests, ask questions, and have conversations with HELM
- #new-workspace — where you describe a new tool or automation you want built
- #capture — drop anything here (links, notes, screenshots) and it's saved forever
- #daily-briefing — where your morning summary appears automatically every day
- #pap-status — ask "is everything working?" any time
- #help — questions, troubleshooting, explanations
- #preferences — change any setting by typing plain English

When HELM builds something for you, it gets its own dedicated channel. Your ETF tracker, your options analyzer, your trip planner — each one lives in its own space with its own history.

---

## BRIEF 3: Workspaces — Custom Tools, Built From Plain English

A "workspace" is what HELM calls the thing it builds for you. That could be:

- A portfolio dashboard that looks exactly how you'd design it, with the columns and filters you care about
- An options analyzer with color-coded alerts, custom calculations, and a mobile-friendly layout
- A weekly report that pulls from sources you choose, formatted exactly your way
- A trip planner that builds detailed itineraries, logs them to your calendar, and delivers summaries anywhere you want
- A data tracker that monitors what matters to you and alerts you the moment it changes

You don't write code. You don't configure anything technical. You describe what you want:

"I want to track my ETF portfolio. Show me each fund's performance yesterday, flag anything that moved more than 2%, and make it look clean enough to share with a friend."

HELM builds it, tests it, and delivers it exactly as described. You keep refining it mid-conversation until it's exactly right.

What makes this different from an app or plugin:
- It's built for your specific workflow, not a generic use case
- You own it completely — code, config, and everything lives on your machine and GitHub
- Outputs can go anywhere: Discord, cloud storage, a custom website on your domain, a drafted email, a calendar event — or all of the above
- You can evolve it mid-conversation without starting over
- The tools HELM produces look and work like professional software — because they are

## BRIEF 4: The Second Brain — HELM Remembers Everything

Every link you drop, article you share, or note you capture in HELM goes into your "second brain" — a searchable, growing knowledge base that belongs entirely to you.

What makes it useful:

- Searchable by meaning, not just keywords. Ask "what did I save about inflation hedging?" and it finds relevant things even if you never used those exact words.
- Connected to everything HELM does. When it's building something, it checks your second brain first.
- Permanent. Unlike browser bookmarks or messaging threads, things in your second brain don't disappear.

Over time, HELM starts noticing patterns in what you save. It surfaces connections you wouldn't have made yourself. This is the part that gets more valuable the longer it runs.

---

## BRIEF 5: HELM Knows What to Ask (and What to Just Do)

Every action HELM takes is automatically classified by stakes:

**Routine work** → HELM does it silently. You never see it happen.

**Visible changes** → HELM acts, then tells you briefly. No question, just a short note.

**Real decisions — consequential or hard to undo** → HELM stops, explains what it's proposing, and waits for your explicit go-ahead.

The result: HELM runs quietly 95% of the time and only interrupts you when interrupting is actually useful. You're never surprised by something you didn't approve, and you're never asked for permission on things that don't need it.

Financial safety is hardcoded, not configurable:
HELM never moves money. It can read financial data (with your permission) but never touches balances.

## BRIEF 6: How HELM Gets Smarter Over Time — Including Its Own Code

HELM doesn't just do the same thing repeatedly. It actively improves itself.

After every build, it captures what worked, what failed, and what it should do differently next time. These learnings go into a shared knowledge base that every future build reads first.

HELM has two meta-level agents — a Product Manager and an Engineer — that work as a continuous improvement loop. The Product Manager observes patterns, identifies the most valuable improvements, and writes fix specs. The Engineer implements them — literally opening bot.js and the agent instruction files and making changes.

The system you have in month 6 has been improved dozens of times since you installed it. You didn't request any of those improvements. They just happened.

## BRIEF 7: The Morning Briefing

Every morning, before you look at your phone, HELM has already:
- Checked your calendar
- Scanned your email for things that matter
- Run any overnight automations
- Identified anything waiting on your decision

It delivers this as a single briefing in #daily-briefing. You open Discord, read one message, and you know exactly where things stand.

You control what goes in it: calendar, email, news topics, weather, active workspace summaries. You set the time, the length, and what to include. And it uses real data — not generic placeholders.
